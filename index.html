Evidence Statement Group Scope Item Status Why Recommended Decision Notes
C1-ES1 Claim Structure Identify whether a claim is descriptive, correlational, or causal. ES Ready No existing scope item asks students to classify the type of claim. This is a foundational skill that parallels “Distinguish between statements of fact, opinion, and inference” but targets claim logic rather than statement category.
C1-ES3 Reasoning Quality Identify when a claim overgeneralizes beyond the evidence. ES Ready Current scope addresses gaps and unsupported assumptions but does not explicitly target overgeneralization — a distinct and common reasoning error where the claim extends beyond what the evidence can justify.
C1-ES3 Evidence Quality Identify when evidence may not represent the broader population. ES Ready Extends the existing “consider whether evidence applies to the population, setting, or context” by making representativeness an explicit, standalone evaluation skill rather than a sub-consideration.
C1-ES3 Data Interpretation Recognize when evidence includes uncertainty or variability. (optional) Formative No current scope item addresses the meta-awareness that data itself can be uncertain or variable. This is harder to assess summatively at Level 2 but important for building honest interpretation habits.

C2 — Supports a Scientific Claim
4 items
Evidence Statement Group Scope Item Status Why Recommended Decision Notes
C2-ES3 Counterclaims and Public Impact Identify a plausible counterclaim. ES Ready C2-ES3 requires refuting a counterclaim, but the current scope has no item for identifying one in the first place. Students need to recognize what a counterclaim is before they can refute it.
C2-ES2 Evidence-to-Claim Reasoning Compare competing claims using evidence. ES Ready Current scope items connect evidence to a single claim but never ask students to weigh evidence across two competing claims — a key reasoning skill for generative argument.
C2-ES2 Evidence-to-Claim Reasoning Explain why one interpretation is more strongly supported than another. ES Ready Follows naturally from comparing claims. Without this, students can compare but are never asked to justify which interpretation wins and why — the core reasoning move of constructive argument.
C2-ES2 Evidence-to-Claim Reasoning Explain how multiple pieces of evidence jointly support a claim. ES Ready Current scope treats evidence-to-claim as one-to-one. Real arguments require synthesizing multiple sources. This fills the gap between “connect evidence to a claim” and building a robust, multi-evidence argument.

3 items
Evidence Statement Group Scope Item Status Why Recommended Decision Notes
C3-ES1 System Boundaries and Components Identify the purpose or function of a system. ES Ready Current scope defines components and boundaries but never asks “what does this system do?” Purpose is the most foundational question about any system and anchors all subsequent analysis.
C3-ES2 Interactions and Feedback Recognize time delays in system effects. Formative Current scope traces change through systems but assumes effects are immediate. Delays are a key dynamic — effects that lag behind their causes — and are critical for understanding feedback loops realistically.

Exploring Systems Like a Scientist

Competency Context Worksheet

Course Level Scope Notes
Systems Perspectives Domain Outcome: Students explore complex systems in the world around them – they analyze interdependencies among peoples, cultures, and environments to apply evidence-based reasoning for sustainable solutions.

Note that it is only the initial part of the outcome that has to be represented completely.

Exploring Systems Like a Scientist introduces science as a way of knowing, focusing on how evidence, logic, and systematic inquiry are used to understand the world and inform decisions. The course emphasizes how scientists ask questions, evaluate evidence, and revise explanations over time, rather than memorizing isolated facts. By exploring unifying scientific ideas in systems, students build the ability to evaluate scientific claims, recognize uncertainty, and apply scientific reasoning to real-world challenges in health, environment, and society.

Key Concepts:

· Inquiry and Reasoning replacing a strict scientific method
· Evaluating Evidence, Data, and Scientific Claims for application
· Uncertainty, Reproducibility, and Peer Review- how to ensure that scientific findings are trustworthy
· Distinguishing Science from Pseudoscience and Misinformation
· Awareness of ideas in major fields of science (e.g., evolution, earth and energy systems, human health, forces and interactions). The specific examples will be aligned to the scenario that the student chooses to follow (see note below)
· Models and Representations in Science – examples should be simple, accessible, relevant
-change in our knowledge – how that shifts models and why this is a strength
· Systems Thinking in Scientific Contexts
· Applying Scientific Reasoning to Societal and Technological Challenges
-Constructing evidence-based explanations

Design Notes: The student is going to pick a scenario and explore all three competencies in the context of that scenario. There should not be a distinct progression of modules from comp 1 to 3. All comps are integrated into their growth of understanding of the nuanced scenario. The course will be constructed so that a student will move through the learning using a specific scenario as context, while other students will be doing the same things but using a different scenario. These parallel paths will all support the same assessment and competencies, but will allow the student to practice these skills using one specific scenario.
Interdisciplinary Components
Analytical & Quantitative Skills: Data evaluation and interpretation
Society, Civic, & Cultural Engagement: development of scientific knowledge, science as a human endeavor, historical and current scientific understanding
Human Thriving: awareness of the world around you and curiosity/desire to engage with. Inquiry as a mindset
Course Level Assessment Strategy and Recommendations
Modality: Performance assessment

Rationale: This course requires learners to engage in inquiry-based reasoning about the world through the lens of science and would be best assessed through the creation of an authentic artifact. All competencies should be assessed at level 2 as they will require explanation and straightforward application.

Artifact Vision:

Choose your own adventure: the student moves through examples that scaffold and grow their ability to evaluate a claim, gather evidence, and consider conclusions. They can choose a track that interests them (some possible ideas listed below). Ultimately, they will write a scientific claim using evidence to support their claim about that concept. This claim should demonstrate:
Make a clear claim
Have evidence to support (could be pulled from the course, or from other sources)
Ability to communicate science concepts including foundational concepts
Ability to connect pieces of a complex system and show how they interact
Scientific considerations, ethical considerations, society impacts

✦ What's New

Cognitive Architecture Revision. Competencies restructured to reflect a clear Evaluate → Support → Explain progression (Knowledge → Skill → Ability). Evidence Statements were refined for Level 2 alignment, single-verb precision, and clean cognitive separation.

Global Structural Revision

New Competency Progression

C1: Evaluates Scientific Claims (Knowledge — Analytical Judgment)
C2: Supports a Scientific Claim (Skill — Constructive Argument)
C3: Explains Real-World Systems (Ability — Systems Application)

Why

The previous structure blended evaluation and construction. The revised model creates a clear cognitive ladder: first judge claims, then build claims, then apply reasoning within systems.

This revision prevents overlap between evaluation (C1) and construction (C2), aligns to Level 2 taxonomy, and creates defensible separation for assessment design.
✏️

C1 — Now Focused on Evaluation

Revised
Old Focus

Mixed support and evaluation language (e.g., determining validity while also selecting evidence).

New Focus

C1 is exclusively about evaluating existing claims — claim structure, evidence quality, reasoning quality, and credibility.

Why

Prevents duplication with C2 and clarifies assessment expectations.

Scope items reorganized into:
  • Claim Structure Recognition
  • Evidence Quality
  • Reasoning Quality
  • Data Interpretation
  • Source Credibility & Bias
Content unchanged — grouping clarified.
🛠

C2 — Now Focused on Construction

Clarified Role
New Focus

C2 centers on making a claim, supporting it with evidence, and refuting a counterclaim — not judging overall validity.

Why

Separates generative reasoning (C2) from evaluative reasoning (C1).

C2 now reflects a constructive argument arc:
  • Make a claim
  • Select and connect evidence
  • Refute opposition
🌐

C3 — Structural Regrouping

No ES Wording Changes
Updated Organization

Evidence Statements unchanged. Scope reorganized into cognitive clusters:

  • System Structure
  • Interactions & Feedback
  • Concept Application
  • Societal & Ethical Dimensions

Why

Clarifies distinction between describing interactions (ES2) and applying scientific concepts (ES3).

📚

Level 2 Rigor Alignment

Verb Audit

All Evidence Statements use a single measurable verb (identify, distinguish, evaluate, make, support, refute, describe, apply).

Why

Ensures Level 2 cognitive demand — explanation and straightforward application — without drifting into advanced statistics, modeling, or philosophical analysis.
