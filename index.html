Exploring Systems Like a Scientist

Competency Context Worksheet

Course Level Scope Notes
Systems Perspectives Domain Outcome: Students explore complex systems in the world around them – they analyze interdependencies among peoples, cultures, and environments to apply evidence-based reasoning for sustainable solutions.

Note that it is only the initial part of the outcome that has to be represented completely.

Exploring Systems Like a Scientist introduces science as a way of knowing, focusing on how evidence, logic, and systematic inquiry are used to understand the world and inform decisions. The course emphasizes how scientists ask questions, evaluate evidence, and revise explanations over time, rather than memorizing isolated facts. By exploring unifying scientific ideas in systems, students build the ability to evaluate scientific claims, recognize uncertainty, and apply scientific reasoning to real-world challenges in health, environment, and society.

Key Concepts:

· Inquiry and Reasoning replacing a strict scientific method
· Evaluating Evidence, Data, and Scientific Claims for application
· Uncertainty, Reproducibility, and Peer Review- how to ensure that scientific findings are trustworthy
· Distinguishing Science from Pseudoscience and Misinformation
· Awareness of ideas in major fields of science (e.g., evolution, earth and energy systems, human health, forces and interactions). The specific examples will be aligned to the scenario that the student chooses to follow (see note below)
· Models and Representations in Science – examples should be simple, accessible, relevant
-change in our knowledge – how that shifts models and why this is a strength
· Systems Thinking in Scientific Contexts
· Applying Scientific Reasoning to Societal and Technological Challenges
-Constructing evidence-based explanations

Design Notes: The student is going to pick a scenario and explore all three competencies in the context of that scenario. There should not be a distinct progression of modules from comp 1 to 3. All comps are integrated into their growth of understanding of the nuanced scenario. The course will be constructed so that a student will move through the learning using a specific scenario as context, while other students will be doing the same things but using a different scenario. These parallel paths will all support the same assessment and competencies, but will allow the student to practice these skills using one specific scenario.
Interdisciplinary Components
Analytical & Quantitative Skills: Data evaluation and interpretation
Society, Civic, & Cultural Engagement: development of scientific knowledge, science as a human endeavor, historical and current scientific understanding
Human Thriving: awareness of the world around you and curiosity/desire to engage with. Inquiry as a mindset
Course Level Assessment Strategy and Recommendations
Modality: Performance assessment

Rationale: This course requires learners to engage in inquiry-based reasoning about the world through the lens of science and would be best assessed through the creation of an authentic artifact. All competencies should be assessed at level 2 as they will require explanation and straightforward application.

Learning resource for this course will be chosen to align with the course’s intent of helping students explore complex systems in the world and analyze interdependencies among people, cultures, and environments to apply evidence-based scientific reasoning for sustainable solutions. C1 — ES Wording Revisions

3 Revised
C1-ES1Revised
expand⌄
Old

The learner determines the validity of a scientific claim based on relevant, credible evidence.

New

The learner selects relevant, credible evidence that supports a scientific claim.

Why

"Selects" replaces "determines validity" — active choice, not final judgment. C1 is about supporting claims, not judging validity.

"Determines validity" implies a final true/false judgment — that's evaluation territory (C2). C1 is about supporting claims with evidence. "Selects" implies active choice and judgment about which evidence to use. It sits at the right Bloom's level: stronger than "identifies" (Level 1, passive recognition) but avoids the evaluative "determines validity" (Level 4+).
Verb Strength Ladder:
• "Identifies" = Level 1 (passive recognition)
• "Selects" = Level 2+ (implies judgment, choosing the RIGHT evidence)
• "Determines validity" = Level 4+ (evaluation/judgment of truth)

"Selects" is the appropriate middle ground for C1's supporting role — students demonstrate judgment through curation, not adjudication.

expand⌄

Old

The learner analyzes societal or ethical considerations related to a scientific claim, including potential benefits, risks, and unintended consequences.

New

The learner explains the potential social or ethical benefits, risks, or consequences of the dissemination of a scientific claim.

"Analyzes" is more evaluative (C2) than supportive (C1). "Explains" fits C1 better. "Dissemination" clarifies the real focus: what happens when claims are shared and communicated.

"Dissemination" clarifies the real focus: what happens when claims are shared and communicated — connecting to misinformation, public health, and policy decisions. The old wording ("related to") was too vague. This reframes the ethical dimension from abstract consideration to concrete consequence — when a scientific claim enters public discourse, what are the stakes?
Aligns with existing scope items:

C1 — New Evidence Statement

1 New
C1-ES2New

New Evidence Statement

The learner constructs a scientific claim using evidence and reasoning.

Why This Was Added

Fills the gap between selecting evidence (ES1) and explaining support (ES3). The CER framework is foundational — students need to build the claim before they can explain the connections.

C1 is about SUPPORTING claims — students need to understand how claims are BUILT to properly support them. The CER (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) Framework is a foundational skill for scientific argumentation. Constructing a claim requires active synthesis, not just recognition or selection.

This creates a logical progression:
Select evidence (ES1) → Build the claim (ES2) → Explain the connection (ES3) → Consider impact (ES4)
Scope items for C1-ES2:
• Constructing a scientific claim
• Using the CER Framework for Constructing a Claim

Note: "Translating scientific language to plain language" moved to ES3 because explaining/articulating requires translation, while constructing does not.
Insertion caused renumbering:
• Old C1-ES2 → now C1-ES3
• Old C1-ES3 → now C1-ES4

C2 — ES Wording Revision

1 Revised
C2-ES3Revised
expand⌄
Old

The learner describes scientific, societal, or ethical considerations related to the claim, its supporting evidence, and the reasoning connecting them.

New

The learner evaluates whether a scientific claim addresses scientific, societal, and ethical considerations, including what evidence is missing, who is affected, and what consequences are unaddressed.

Why

"Describes" was too neutral and passive — just listing factors. "Evaluates whether... including what is missing" requires critical analysis: finding gaps, not just listing. This is distinctly evaluative, clearly belonging in C2.

Students must judge the quality of an argument by identifying omissions. To find what's missing, they must first understand what should be there — building the mental model this Knowledge Graph visualizes.

Comparison of student responses:

OLD response: "This claim has societal considerations like public health and access. Ethical considerations include informed consent."

NEW response: "This claim doesn't address how the treatment affects elderly populations. It also ignores the ethical issue of affordability."
To identify what's MISSING, students must first understand what SHOULD BE THERE. This requires building a mental model of a complete, well-supported claim:

• Claim
• Evidence (relevant, credible, sufficient)
• Reasoning connecting evidence to claim
• Scientific considerations (mechanism, controls, sample size)
• Societal considerations (who's affected, context, applications)
• Ethical considerations (consequences, trade-offs, fairness)

Teaching Sequence:
1. Teach the complete structure (knowledge graph of a well-supported claim)
2. Students internalize this mental model of completeness
3. On the PA, students compare the given claim against their mental model
4. They identify and explain gaps — what's missing from the complete picture
