Exploring Systems Like a Scientist

Competency Context Worksheet

Course Level Scope Notes
Systems Perspectives Domain Outcome: Students explore complex systems in the world around them – they analyze interdependencies among peoples, cultures, and environments to apply evidence-based reasoning for sustainable solutions.

Note that it is only the initial part of the outcome that has to be represented completely.

Exploring Systems Like a Scientist introduces science as a way of knowing, focusing on how evidence, logic, and systematic inquiry are used to understand the world and inform decisions. The course emphasizes how scientists ask questions, evaluate evidence, and revise explanations over time, rather than memorizing isolated facts. By exploring unifying scientific ideas in systems, students build the ability to evaluate scientific claims, recognize uncertainty, and apply scientific reasoning to real-world challenges in health, environment, and society.

Key Concepts:

· Inquiry and Reasoning replacing a strict scientific method
· Evaluating Evidence, Data, and Scientific Claims for application
· Uncertainty, Reproducibility, and Peer Review- how to ensure that scientific findings are trustworthy
· Distinguishing Science from Pseudoscience and Misinformation
· Awareness of ideas in major fields of science (e.g., evolution, earth and energy systems, human health, forces and interactions). The specific examples will be aligned to the scenario that the student chooses to follow (see note below)
· Models and Representations in Science – examples should be simple, accessible, relevant
-change in our knowledge – how that shifts models and why this is a strength
· Systems Thinking in Scientific Contexts
· Applying Scientific Reasoning to Societal and Technological Challenges
-Constructing evidence-based explanations

Design Notes: The student is going to pick a scenario and explore all three competencies in the context of that scenario. There should not be a distinct progression of modules from comp 1 to 3. All comps are integrated into their growth of understanding of the nuanced scenario. The course will be constructed so that a student will move through the learning using a specific scenario as context, while other students will be doing the same things but using a different scenario. These parallel paths will all support the same assessment and competencies, but will allow the student to practice these skills using one specific scenario.
Interdisciplinary Components
Analytical & Quantitative Skills: Data evaluation and interpretation
Society, Civic, & Cultural Engagement: development of scientific knowledge, science as a human endeavor, historical and current scientific understanding
Human Thriving: awareness of the world around you and curiosity/desire to engage with. Inquiry as a mindset
Course Level Assessment Strategy and Recommendations
Modality: Performance assessment

Rationale: This course requires learners to engage in inquiry-based reasoning about the world through the lens of science and would be best assessed through the creation of an authentic artifact. All competencies should be assessed at level 2 as they will require explanation and straightforward application.

Potential Evidence of Competency at Specified Level:

WBL Considerations:

Artifact Vision:

Choose your own adventure: the student moves through examples that scaffold and grow their ability to evaluate a claim, gather evidence, and consider conclusions. They can choose a track that interests them (some possible ideas listed below). Ultimately, they will write a scientific claim using evidence to support their claim about that concept. This claim should demonstrate:
Make a clear claim
Have evidence to support (could be pulled from the course, or from other sources)
Ability to communicate science concepts including foundational concepts
Ability to connect pieces of a complex system and show how they interact
Scientific considerations, ethical considerations, society impacts
Scientific ethics – what are the impacts to the system (body, community, earth, all levels)
Demonstrate understanding of the scientific concepts associated

Static videos of someone making claims and they have to respond in real time (pause and respond tool)
Push towards validating or invalidating (or maybe, this is nuanced)
Even if they agree, they need to provide evidence for their reasoning
Identify hidden assumptions, misinformation, bias
Are they demonstrating a systems perspective
Multiple clips, each one asking them to watch for one specific thing
What assumptions/biases are being made
What can be validated or invalidated about the claim
What aspects of the system are not being recognized
All of the responses are not about finding the ‘right’ answer, but instead focusing on the process and right way to think and communicate
Time limit (ten minutes? We do not need this to be a polished written response)
open resource (they can use notes, web, etc)

Possible nuanced scenarios to explore in the course and for the assessment
Invasive species
Fire suppression
Urban green spaces and community health
Antibiotic resistance
Hygiene hypothesis
Stress as toxic or adaptive
Recycling
GMOs in agriculture
Course Level LR Strategy and Recommendations
Learning resource for this course will be chosen to align with the course’s intent of helping students explore complex systems in the world and analyze interdependencies among people, cultures, and environments to apply evidence-based scientific reasoning for sustainable solutions. LRs will emphasize real world scenarios and applications, as well as a variety of evidence-based strategies to help students develop a scientific framework.

A variety of resources such as textbooks, scholarly sources, multimedia resources, case studies, and scenarios will be used with emphasis on experiential, real-world, and interactive learning where possible.
